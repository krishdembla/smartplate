\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Food classification and detection.}
Early work treated food understanding as whole-image classification.
Food-101~\cite{bossard2014food101} introduced the canonical benchmark with $101$ dish categories and $1{,}000$ images per class and has driven deep-network baselines for over a decade.
UECFood-100 and UECFood-256~\cite{matsuda2012uecfood} added bounding-box annotations for multi-item trays.
Both families treat each dish as an atomic unit, which works for restaurant menus but poorly for home-cooked plates where a single serving is itself a mixture of ingredients.
SmartPlate targets this mixed-plate setting and therefore operates at ingredient level rather than dish level.

\paragraph{Food segmentation.}
Pixel-level food understanding started with small, single-cuisine datasets and became tractable at scale with FoodSeg103~\cite{wu2021foodseg103}, which covers $103$ ingredient classes with dense pixel masks across $7{,}118$ images.
Stronger backbones such as DeepLabV3+~\cite{chen2018deeplabv3plus} have raised the bar on this benchmark, and more recent work like FoodSAM~\cite{lan2023foodsam} composes the Segment Anything Model~\cite{kirillov2023sam} with a food classifier to support prompted segmentation.
We fine-tune SegFormer~\cite{xie2021segformer} on FoodSeg103; its hierarchical MiT encoder and lightweight all-MLP decoder give a favorable accuracy-to-parameter ratio and train in under an hour on a single T4 GPU.

\paragraph{Nutrition estimation.}
Existing nutrition-estimation pipelines fall into two camps.
Regression-based approaches, typified by Nutrition5k~\cite{thames2021nutrition5k}, use RGB-D recordings paired with ground-truth ingredient masses to regress macros directly; these can be accurate but require depth sensors and tend to generalize poorly outside the training distribution.
Rule-based pipelines instead identify ingredients via classification or segmentation and look up a reference table~\cite{usdafdc}.
SmartPlate follows the second approach: proportions come from a single RGB image, macros are looked up per group from USDA FoodData Central~\cite{usdafdc}, and the final score is a transparent function of those proportions.

\paragraph{Dietary scoring rubrics.}
Mapping plate composition to a scalar health score has a long history in nutrition epidemiology, most notably through the Healthy Eating Index used in USDA dietary surveys.
We adopt the Harvard Healthy Eating Plate~\cite{harvardplate} because its visual prescription---half the plate vegetables and fruits, a quarter whole grains, a quarter healthy protein---maps directly onto per-class pixel-area proportions from a segmentation model.
To our knowledge, this pairing of a publicly available dietary rubric with a laptop-scale food segmenter has not been reported in prior computer-vision work.
